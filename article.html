Why we still hand-baste every lapel

Jun 18, 2026 — 4 min read

A machine can sew a lapel roll in under a minute. Our tailors still do it by hand, and it takes most of an afternoon. Here's why that hasn't changed.

Hand-basting is the temporary stitch that holds a lapel's roll in place while it's pressed and shaped for the first time. It's meant to come out. Most houses skip it, or replace it with a fused interlining that holds the shape permanently, mechanically, without a person making a judgment call.

The problem with fusing is that it decides the lapel's shape once, on a flat table, before the jacket has ever been worn. A hand-basted lapel is shaped on a stand, checked against how the canvas underneath actually wants to sit, adjusted, and checked again. It's slower because it's a conversation instead of an instruction.

What it actually changes

In practice, a hand-basted lapel breaks in rather than breaking down. The roll softens with wear instead of cracking at the fold line, which is the thing that ages a fused jacket badly after a few years. It's a small, invisible decision that only shows up later — which is exactly the kind of decision Sync tends to make.

We're not precious about doing everything by hand. Plenty of our construction is machine-sewn, and machine work is often more consistent than hand work. But basting is one of a handful of steps where the slower method produces a genuinely different, better result — not just a more expensive-looking one.

Where you'll find it

Every jacket and coat in the Structure and Movement collections is hand-basted before its first press. It's not listed as a feature on the product page, because it isn't really a feature — it's just how the lapel is supposed to be made.
